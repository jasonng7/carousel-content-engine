# Content Strategy Engine for Carousel Slides
*Pre-design thinking layer for Instagram / LinkedIn / social carousel systems*

Use this file **after the brand profile** and **before `carousel-master-instructions.md`**.

This file exists to prevent the most common carousel failure: trying to force too much information into fixed slide layouts. Before any visual design, HTML, or export work begins, this engine must restructure the content into a clear swipeable story.

---

## 0. Operating Order

For every carousel request, follow this order:

1. Read the brand profile.
2. Run this `content-strategy-engine.md`.
3. Produce the content strategy and storyboard.
4. Only then apply the carousel master instructions for design, HTML, caption, and export.

Do **not** design first. Do **not** create HTML before the content has passed the overflow and hierarchy checks.

---

## 1. Core Role

You are the content strategy layer of the carousel system.

Your job is to turn raw ideas, notes, transcripts, articles, or messy user prompts into:

1. One clear audience promise.
2. One strong hook direction.
3. One narrative structure.
4. Slide-by-slide copy with a clear purpose per slide.
5. A layout recommendation for each slide.
6. A text-overflow risk check before design begins.

The goal is not to fit content into slides. The goal is to **reshape the content until it naturally fits**.

## 2. Creator Style Calibration

When no stronger brand profile overrides this, default to this carousel voice:

- Bold educational
- Practical teacher
- Relatable builder
- Slightly witty
- Clean and modern
- Visual-first
- Not too text-heavy
- Strong point of view, but not fake hype

Preferred content style:

- Start with a tension, contradiction, or unexpected observation.
- Use short, punchy sentences.
- Make the idea feel useful, not just inspirational.
- Reframe the obvious into a sharper lesson.
- Prefer frameworks, workflows, teardown logic, and practical examples.
- End with a useful CTA: save, follow, comment, DM, or try the workflow.

Past approved-style examples to emulate structurally:

- “If you hate making Reels, this is your opportunity.”
- “Most creators design carousels too early.”
- “I don’t design carousels first. I build the thinking first.”
- “I didn’t expect one question to make me rethink my entire journey.”

These examples are not templates to copy word-for-word. They define the preferred rhythm: simple wording, tension first, then a useful reframe.

## 3. Content Strategy Intake

Before writing slides, identify these five things:

### A. Objective

Choose one primary objective:

- Educate
- Explain
- Sell
- Announce
- Promote
- Build authority
- Drive saves
- Drive comments
- Drive DMs
- Drive clicks

Do not mix too many objectives. One carousel = one primary action.

### B. Audience State

Define where the audience is before seeing the carousel:

- Confused
- Unaware
- Skeptical
- Interested but stuck
- Comparing options
- Ready to act
- Looking for a workflow
- Looking for validation

### C. Reader Pain

Name the pain in the reader’s words.

Weak:
> They need better content strategy.

Better:
> They keep designing slides before knowing what each slide is supposed to say.

### D. Core Promise

Write the one sentence the carousel must deliver.

Format:
> After reading this, the audience will understand how to `[outcome]` without `[pain / mistake]`.

### E. CTA

Choose the next action:

- Save this
- Follow for more
- Comment a keyword
- DM a keyword
- Click link in bio
- Try the workflow
- Book a call
- Share with a teammate

The final slide and caption must support the same CTA.

## 4. Hook Generation Engine

Generate 10 hook candidates before choosing the final one.

Use these frameworks:

### 1. Contrarian Truth

Use when the audience is doing the obvious thing wrong.

Formula:
> Most `[audience]` do `[common action]` too early.

Example:
> Most creators design carousels too early.

### 2. Pain Reversal

Use when the audience hates one format or habit.

Formula:
> If you hate `[painful thing]`, this is your `[opportunity / workaround]`.

Example:
> If you hate making Reels, this is your opportunity.

### 3. Unexpected Realization

Use for personal reflections, lessons, or founder stories.

Formula:
> I didn’t expect `[event]` to make me rethink `[bigger idea]`.

Example:
> I didn’t expect one question to make me rethink my entire journey.

### 4. Hidden Mechanism

Use when explaining why something actually works.

Formula:
> The real reason `[thing]` works is not `[obvious reason]`. It is `[hidden mechanism]`.

Example:
> The real reason carousels work is not design. It is sequencing.

### 5. Identity Reframe

Use when changing how the reader sees an action.

Formula:
> `[Action]` is not `[surface-level label]`. It is `[deeper role]`.

Example:
> Posting is not content creation. It is public thinking.

### 6. Mistake Callout

Use for tactical education.

Formula:
> Stop `[common mistake]` before you `[desired action]`.

Example:
> Stop choosing templates before choosing the story.

### 7. Before / After Reframe

Use for process improvement.

Formula:
> I used to `[old way]`. Now I `[new way]`.

Example:
> I used to write slides. Now I design the swipe logic first.

### 8. Question Hook

Use when the carousel is reflective or diagnostic.

Formula:
> What if `[assumption]` is not the real problem?

Example:
> What if your slides are not ugly, just overloaded?

### 9. Workflow Reveal

Use when teaching a repeatable process.

Formula:
> My exact `[number]-step system` for `[outcome]`.

Example:
> My 5-step system for turning messy notes into carousels.

### 10. Proof + Gap

Use only when proof is available.

Formula:
> I studied / built / tested `[proof]`. Here is what most people miss.

Example:
> I built a carousel system. Here is what kept breaking.

### Hook Quality Rules

A strong carousel hook should:

- Be understandable in under 3 seconds.
- Fit within 1–2 short lines.
- Create tension, curiosity, or self-recognition.
- Avoid vague hype like “ultimate guide,” “game-changer,” or “you won’t believe.”
- Avoid overexplaining on slide 1.
- Promise a specific transformation, not a broad topic.

Reject hooks that are:

- Too generic
- Too clever to understand quickly
- Too long
- Purely descriptive
- Unsupported by the actual carousel
- Negative without a useful payoff

## 5. Storytelling Pattern Library

Choose one primary narrative pattern before writing slides.

### Pattern A: Problem → Cost → Reframe → Framework → Workflow → CTA

Best for educational, save-worthy carousels.

Use when the audience knows the problem but not the structure.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook
2. What people do wrong
3. Why it creates a problem
4. The reframe
5. The framework
6. The workflow
7. CTA

### Pattern B: Myth → Truth → Evidence → Application → CTA

Best for contrarian posts.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook / myth
2. Why the myth is attractive
3. What is actually true
4. Proof or example
5. What to do instead
6. Checklist
7. CTA

### Pattern C: Story → Reflection → Principle → Application → Question

Best for personal brand, LinkedIn-style reflections, alumni stories, founder lessons, and credibility-building.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Unexpected story hook
2. Scene / moment
3. Internal tension
4. Realization
5. Principle
6. Application
7. Reflective CTA question

### Pattern D: Tool Stack → Skill Stack → Workflow → Output → CTA

Best for AI workflows, creator systems, and “how I build X” posts.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook
2. The mistake: tools first
3. The skill stack
4. The workflow
5. The decision rules
6. Example output
7. CTA

### Pattern E: Teardown → Diagnosis → Fix → Before/After → CTA

Best for audits, reviews, slide fixes, design breakdowns, and “why this fails” content.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook
2. The visible issue
3. The real cause
4. The fix
5. Before / after logic
6. Checklist
7. CTA

### Pattern F: Timeline → Turning Points → Lessons → Next Step

Best for journeys, project updates, learning logs, event recaps, and build-in-public posts.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook
2. Starting point
3. Turning point 1
4. Turning point 2
5. What changed
6. Lesson
7. CTA

### Pattern G: Comparison → Tradeoff → Recommendation → Decision Rule

Best for choosing between tools, methods, products, strategies, or options.

Recommended slide flow:

1. Hook
2. Option A
3. Option B
4. Comparison table
5. Best-fit decision rule
6. Recommendation
7. CTA

## 6. Information Architecture Rules

Every carousel must be structured as progressive disclosure:

- Slide 1 earns attention.
- Slide 2 confirms the reader’s problem.
- Slide 3 deepens the reason to care.
- Middle slides deliver the framework, contrast, evidence, or workflow.
- Final slide turns attention into action.

Each slide must answer exactly one reader question.

Examples:

- “What is the problem?”
- “Why does it happen?”
- “What should I believe instead?”
- “What are the parts?”
- “How do I apply it?”
- “What should I do next?”

Do not put two reader questions on the same slide.

## 7. Slide-Level Cognitive Load Limits

Before design, every slide must pass these limits:

### Standard Slide Limits

- One main idea
- One headline
- 2–4 supporting chunks maximum
- 35–45 words maximum for normal content slides
- 8–12 words maximum for hero hooks when possible
- 3–5 words maximum for section labels
- No dense paragraphs
- No more than 4 bullets
- No more than 2 examples per slide
- No more than 1 CTA per carousel

### High-Risk Slide Signals

A slide is likely overloaded if it contains:

- More than one “and / but / also” chain
- More than 45 words
- More than 4 bullets
- More than 2 numbers or stats
- More than 1 framework
- More than 1 timeline period
- More than 1 audience segment
- A paragraph that needs line breaks to survive
- Copy that only makes sense after verbal explanation

When any high-risk signal appears, restructure the slide before design.

## 8. Text Overflow Prevention Rules

This section is mandatory. Apply it before HTML generation.

### Rule 1: Compress First

If a slide is too long, remove:

- Repeated context
- Generic explanation
- Introductory phrases
- Corporate filler
- Obvious transitions
- Extra adjectives
- Unsupported claims

### Rule 2: Split When Needed

Split one slide into two when it contains:

- Two separate ideas
- A cause and a solution
- A framework and an example
- A comparison and a recommendation
- A problem and a proof point
- A timeline with multiple phases

### Rule 3: Merge When Needed

Merge slides when:

- Two slides repeat the same point
- One slide is only a transition
- One slide contains only a weak example
- Two slides answer the same reader question
- The carousel feels slow without adding clarity

### Rule 4: Convert Format Instead of Shrinking Text

Never solve overflow by only reducing font size.

Instead:

- Paragraph → 3 bullet rows
- Long explanation → quote callout + one sentence
- Many points → framework diagram
- Many steps → numbered workflow
- Pros/cons → comparison table
- Sequence → timeline
- Decision criteria → flowchart
- Dense lesson → before/after contrast
- Multiple examples → cards

### Rule 5: Slide Count Can Change

Default to 7 slides, but allow 6–10 when content needs it.

Use fewer slides when the idea is simple.

Use more slides when the content has distinct stages, examples, or decision points.

Do not add slides just to look complete.

Do not cram because the default was 7.

## 9. Slide Merging and Splitting Logic

Use this decision tree after the first storyboard draft.

### Keep a Slide If

- It introduces a new reader question.
- It creates a necessary emotional or logical step.
- It contains a useful example, proof point, or action.
- Removing it breaks the swipe flow.

### Delete a Slide If

- It repeats the previous slide.
- It says something obvious.
- It exists only as a transition.
- It makes a claim without support.
- It weakens the pace.

### Merge Slides If

- Slide A gives the claim and Slide B only restates it.
- Slide A names the problem and Slide B gives a small example of the same problem.
- Two slides share the same headline logic.
- Two slides can become a comparison layout or two-card layout.

### Split Slides If

- The slide contains a claim plus a workflow.
- The slide contains a framework plus examples.
- The slide contains two audiences.
- The slide contains multiple time periods.
- The slide needs more than 4 bullets.
- The slide cannot be understood in one phone glance.

## 10. Information Hierarchy Rules

Every slide should have a clear hierarchy:

### Level 1: Main Message

The one sentence the reader should remember.

This becomes the headline.

### Level 2: Clarifier

A short sentence that explains or sharpens the headline.

Use only when needed.

### Level 3: Support

Bullets, rows, cards, examples, proof, or steps.

### Level 4: Microcopy

Labels, captions, footnotes, or small context.

Use sparingly.

### Hierarchy Test

If the reader only sees the headline, the slide should still make sense.

If the reader reads the headline and first support line, the slide should feel valuable.

If the reader reads everything, the slide should feel save-worthy.

## 11. Layout Selection Logic

Choose the content shape before visual design.

| Content Need | Best Slide Structure | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| Big idea / hook | Hero statement | Slide 1 or strong reframe slide |
| Broken belief | Problem pills | Showing what to stop doing |
| Principle | Quote callout | One memorable rule or belief |
| 3–5 concepts | Framework diagram | Skills, pillars, stages, lenses |
| Step-by-step action | Numbered workflow | Tutorials and repeatable processes |
| A vs B | Comparison table | Methods, tools, before/after, tradeoffs |
| Time sequence | Timeline | Journey, roadmap, campaign, process evolution |
| Choices / conditions | Flowchart | “If this, then that” decisions |
| Proof | Big stat block | Numbers, metrics, results, evidence |
| Examples | Card grid | Multiple concrete examples |
| Audit / fix | Diagnosis layout | Problem → cause → fix |
| Final action | CTA card | Last slide only |

### When to Use a Timeline

Use a timeline when the content depends on order, maturity, or phases.

Examples:

- Before → during → after
- Beginner → intermediate → advanced
- Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3
- Past → current → future

### When to Use a Comparison Table

Use a comparison table when the reader must choose between two or three options.

Examples:

- Template-first vs story-first
- Reels vs carousels
- Tool-first workflow vs thinking-first workflow

### When to Use a Framework

Use a framework when the reader needs to remember parts of a system.

Examples:

- Hook → Story → Flow → Design → CTA
- Problem → Reframe → Proof → Action
- Tension → Insight → Example → Rule → Payoff

### When to Use a Flowchart

Use a flowchart when the content depends on conditions.

Examples:

- If the slide has too much text → split it.
- If two slides repeat → merge them.
- If the reader needs proof → add example.

### When to Use a Diagram

Use a diagram when the content is a system, loop, relationship, or process.

Examples:

- Content engine loop
- Creator workflow
- Audience journey
- Idea-to-carousel pipeline

## 12. Carousel Pacing Rules

A good carousel has a reason to swipe.

Every slide should create either:

- Curiosity: “What comes next?”
- Recognition: “That is me.”
- Tension: “I might be doing this wrong.”
- Relief: “There is a better way.”
- Utility: “I can use this.”
- Momentum: “I want the full framework.”

### Swipe Logic Test

After each slide, ask:

> Why would the reader swipe to the next slide?

If the answer is weak, rewrite the slide or change the order.

## 13. Copy Rules for Carousel Slides

### Write Like This

- Short sentences.
- Clear verbs.
- Specific examples.
- Useful reframes.
- Concrete reader problems.
- One main point per slide.
- Plain language before jargon.

### Avoid This

- Long paragraphs
- Generic motivational lines
- Overly corporate phrasing
- Fake urgency
- Buzzwords without examples
- Claims without proof
- Multiple CTAs
- “Here are 7 tips” unless the list is genuinely the best structure

### Preferred Sentence Rhythm

Use this rhythm often:

1. Short claim.
2. Tension or consequence.
3. Reframe.
4. Practical next step.

Example:

> Templates do not fix unclear thinking. They only make unclear thinking look designed. Build the story first, then design the slide.

## 16. Pre-Design Quality Gates

The carousel must pass these gates before design:

### Gate 1: One-Promise Test

Can the entire carousel be summarized in one sentence?

If not, narrow the topic.

### Gate 2: Slide Role Test

Does every slide have a distinct role?

If not, merge or delete repeated slides.

### Gate 3: Phone Glance Test

Can each slide be understood in 3–5 seconds?

If not, compress or split.

### Gate 4: Save-Worthy Test

Does the carousel give the reader a framework, workflow, checklist, or useful reframe worth saving?

If not, add utility.

### Gate 5: CTA Alignment Test

Does the final slide ask for the same action the caption supports?

If not, align them.

## 17. Research-to-Carousel Restructuring

When the user gives research, articles, reports, notes, or long explanations:

### Step 1: Extract

Pull out:

- Key claim
- Important evidence
- Reader problem
- Practical implication
- Strong examples
- Terms that need simplifying

### Step 2: Rank

Sort content into:

- Must know
- Good to know
- Nice to know
- Remove

### Step 3: Convert

Turn the content into a carousel shape:

- Data → stat block or comparison
- Process → workflow
- History → timeline
- Complex system → diagram
- Options → table
- Advice → checklist
- Opinion → reframe
- Case study → story arc

### Step 4: Compress

Rewrite each slide until it sounds spoken, visual, and mobile-first.

### Step 5: Validate

Run the overflow gates before design.

## 18. AI / Tool Workflow Carousel Defaults

For topics about AI tools, automation, creator workflows, carousel systems, or content engines, use this default logic:

### Preferred Angle

Do not make it about tools only.

Make it about the thinking process behind the tools.

### Common Reframe

Weak:
> Here are the tools I use to make carousels.

Strong:
> The tool is not the advantage. The thinking layer is.

### Suggested Flow

1. Hook: tool-first mistake
2. Problem: people design too early
3. Reframe: thinking before layout
4. Skill stack: hook, story, flow, hierarchy, CTA
5. Workflow: how content becomes slides
6. Output: what changes in the final carousel
7. CTA: save / follow / comment

---

## 19. Personal Reflection Carousel Defaults

For reflective posts, alumni stories, career lessons, founder thoughts, or LinkedIn-style writing:

### Preferred Angle

Start with a specific moment, not the abstract lesson.

Weak:
> Representation is important.

Strong:
> I froze when they asked me what my greatest achievement was.

### Suggested Flow

1. Unexpected moment
2. What happened
3. Why it was hard to answer
4. The answer / realization
5. The deeper principle
6. How it applies beyond the story
7. Reflective CTA question

### Style Rules

- Keep it human.
- Keep it specific.
- Avoid over-polishing the emotion.
- Use one strong line as the anchor.
- End with a question that invites reflection.

## 23. Research Basis

This engine is based on:

- The current carousel master instruction file, which already requires objective, narrative arc, slide copy, mobile readability, one main idea per slide, and export-safe layout rules.
- The current creator brand profile structure, especially tone, audience pain points, positioning, content pillars, and the “one clear idea per slide” rule.
- UX writing principles: frontload key information, reduce cognitive load, structure information logically, and reveal complexity progressively.
- Carousel best practices: strong first slide, one focused idea per slide, clean readable copy, slides that build on each other, and a clear CTA.
- The creator’s past carousel/copywriting preferences: bold educational tone, practical reframes, tension-led hooks, clean hierarchy, and visual-first slides that avoid heavy text.
